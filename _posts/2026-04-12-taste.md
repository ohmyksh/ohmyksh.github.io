Everyone talks about taste. In an era where AI surveys papers, runs experiments, and writes code faster than any human, the ability to judge which problems are worth pursuing is becoming the core skill for researchers.

I just graduated and am taking my first steps in research. I know taste matters, and I want to build my own. But the harder question is: how do I actually develop it?

<div style="height:30px;"></div>

AI systems work well when the search space is defined, when the seed node is already set. AI mostly learns from trajectories after the search is done. But in reality, most of the work happens through direct exploration across a huge space, going through many failures, and none of that gets stored.

It might seem like problem definition is the only thing left for humans. But it is not just "left over." Problem definition is the most critical part of problem solving. And it comes down to taste.

There is no ground truth for taste. How do we know which research will matter before it happens? The space is undefined, there is no objective to maximize, and taste is hard to frame as optimization. Why does this problem matter and not that one? Why is one direction less meaningful and another more so? Pruning, narrowing, choosing the seed node. That is what matters most. And the more search experience one accumulates, the better the strategies get and the stronger the intuition. Developing taste, in the end, is the process of finding one's own objective function inside a vast and fast-expanding space.

<div style="height:30px;"></div>

Taste is how you differentiate yourself in the post-AGI era. Here is my personal approach:

I am looking for problems I genuinely care about and find exciting, and as part of that, I try to understand what other people think the important problems are. Right now my research is centered around information retrieval (IR), which keeps pulling me into conversations across very different fields. The method that works for me is just talking to people.

Since my third year of undergrad, when I was trying to find my own solid reasons for going to a PhD program, I have had over 150 one-on-one conversations. Now in grad school, I have at least one a week. The only filter is "AI + X" and the field does not matter. If anything, people far from my area tend to give me the most unexpected insights.

In these conversations, I try to interview others about their taste and how it formed over time. What are they interested in right now, and what motivated them to get there? By what criteria did they make their choices, and why do they think those choices matter? What is the bigger picture they are working toward? Hamming famously asked what the most important problems in your field are and why you are not working on them. Andy Matuschak flips it: "What are the most interesting (not important!) problems in your field? Why aren't you working on them?" I keep turning both questions over in my head.

Why does this help with taste? Supervised learning trains on labels that are already fixed. I prefer an approach closer to reinforcement learning: interacting with the environment directly and updating from feedback. For the domains I cannot experience myself, I explore them indirectly through the mental models of people who have. As Hamming put it, keeping the door open and repeating the cycle of action and feedback is what I think matters most.

Looking back on my life so far, in high school I learned what it means to ask questions actively. In college I trained on questions that had already been solved and practiced the process of answering them. Now I think I am at the stage of figuring out what my own questions are, how to work through them, and how to convince others that those questions and answers are worth taking seriously. So while conversations with others are a constant, they do not determine what I think. If anything, the more I talk to people, the more I realize how important it is to have questions that are truly my own. What I am ultimately trying to do is find my own good questions, the ones I find genuinely interesting. But exploration alone is not enough. To carve out a field of my own, I need to exploit as well. I hope to write about this next.

AI can learn and execute fast from what already exists. I prefer to explore wide and deep, slowly, building my own taste along the way.
